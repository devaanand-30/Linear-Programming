Transportation Method - Steps to Solve

The Transportation Model is used to determine the most cost-effective way to distribute products from several suppliers to several consumers while satisfying supply and demand constraints.

Steps to Solve a Transportation Problem

  1. Formulate the Problem:
    • Supply and Demand:
      • List the supply capacity of each source (e.g., factories).
      • List the demand requirements of each destination (e.g., warehouses).
    • Create the Cost Matrix:
      • Input the transportation cost per unit from each source to each destination.
  2. Check for Balance:
    • Balanced Problem: Total Supply equals Total Demand.
    • Unbalanced Problem: Add a dummy source or destination to balance the problem.
  3. Initialize the Solution Using One of the Methods:
    • North-West Corner Method
    • Least Cost Method
    • Vogel's Approximation Method (VAM)
  4. Create the Initial Allocation Table:
    • Record the allocations made during initialization.
    • Ensure that all supply and demand requirements are met.
  5. Test the Initial Solution for Optimality:
    • Use the Modified Distribution Method (MODI) or the Stepping Stone Method.
    • Calculate the opportunity cost for each empty cell.
    • Identify if there is a possibility to reduce the total cost.
  6. Adjust the Solution:
    • Find a closed path and adjust allocations.
    • Update the allocations to reduce total cost.
    • Re-calculate opportunity costs and repeat the optimality test if necessary.
  7. Determine the Optimal Solution:
    • Calculate the total transportation cost.
    • Present the final transportation plan.

Example Problem

Problem Statement:

A company needs to transport goods from three factories to three warehouses. The supply at each factory, the demand at each warehouse, and the shipping costs per unit are given below.

Supply:

  • Factory F1: 20 units
  • Factory F2: 40 units
  • Factory F3: 40 units

Demand:

  • Warehouse W1: 30 units
  • Warehouse W2: 25 units
  • Warehouse W3: 45 units

Cost Matrix:

W1 (\$2) W2 (\$3) W3 (\$11)
F1
F2 \$6 \$1 \$9
F3 \$10 \$5 \$8

Steps to Solve Using the Least Cost Method

  1. Identify the Cell with the Lowest Cost:

    The lowest cost is \$1 (F2 → W2).

  2. Allocate as Much as Possible:

    Allocate min(40,25) = 25 units to F2 → W2.

    Update Supply and Demand:

    • F2 Supply left: \( 40 - 25 = 15 \)
    • W2 Demand met: \( 25 - 25 = 0 \)
  3. Repeat the Process:

    Next lowest cost is \$2 (F1 → W1).

    Allocate min(20,30) = 20 units to F1 → W1.

    Update Supply and Demand:

    • F1 Supply exhausted: \( 20 - 20 = 0 \)
    • W1 Demand left: \( 30 - 20 = 10 \)
  4. Continue Allocations:

    Next lowest cost is \$3 (F1 → W2), but F1 is exhausted.

    Next is \$5 (F3 → W2), but W2 demand is met.

    Allocate to F2 → W1 (\$6): min(15,10) = 10 units.

    Update Supply and Demand:

    • F2 Supply left: \( 15 - 10 = 5 \)
    • W1 Demand met: \( 10 - 10 = 0 \)
  5. Finalize Allocations:

    Allocate remaining F2 supply to F2 → W3 (\$9): 5 units.

    Allocate remaining F3 supply to F3 → W3 (\$8): 40 units.

Compute Total Transportation Cost

\( \text{Total Cost} = (20 \times \$2) + (10 \times \$6) + (25 \times \$1) + (5 \times \$9) + (40 \times \$8) = \$490 \)

Interpretation

The company should transport goods as per the allocations to minimize transportation costs. The Least Cost Method provided an efficient initial solution.
